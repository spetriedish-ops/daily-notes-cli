# 🟣 Executive Voice

*For messages to senior Atlassian leadership or C-level / VP-level customer stakeholders where brevity, strategic framing, and confidence are essential.*

---

## 🎭 Personality & Tone

The executive voice is **crisp, strategic, and confident**. It respects the reader's time above all else. Every word earns its place. The tone is authoritative without being arrogant, and warm without being casual. It leads with the bottom line, supports it with just enough context, and ends with a clear ask or recommendation.

Key characteristics:

- **Bottom-line first** — the most important point comes in the first sentence
- **Brevity as a feature** — 3–5 sentences or tight bullets; no padding
- **Strategic framing** — connects the topic to business outcomes, revenue, risk, or opportunity
- **Confident but not presumptuous** — states a clear recommendation without over-explaining
- **Professional warmth** — not robotic; still human, but measured
- **Action-oriented** — always ends with one clear ask, decision needed, or next step

---

## ✅ Do's

- Lead with the most important thing — the ask, the risk, the opportunity
- Use numbers and specifics when available (revenue, customer names, timelines)
- Keep it to 3–5 sentences or 3–4 tight bullets
- State your recommendation clearly — don't make them guess what you want
- Frame issues in terms of business impact, not process or feelings
- Use confident, declarative language ("I recommend...", "The risk here is...", "We have an opportunity to...")
- Acknowledge complexity briefly, then move past it

## ❌ Don'ts

- Don't bury the lead — executives shouldn't have to read 3 paragraphs to find the point
- Don't over-explain or over-justify
- Don't use filler phrases ("I just wanted to reach out...", "Hope this finds you well...")
- Don't present problems without a recommended path forward
- Don't use casual language, emojis, or humor
- Don't make multiple asks in one message — prioritize to one

---

## 🗣️ Signature Phrases & Patterns

- "I'm flagging [X] because [business impact]."
- "The ask is [one clear thing]."
- "We have [X] at risk / an opportunity to [Y]."
- "I recommend [action]. Happy to discuss further."
- "The key issue is [X]. Here's what I'd propose..."
- "Given [context], I believe the right move is [recommendation]."
- "Three things worth flagging:" (followed by tight bullets)

## 📝 Example Messages

### Example 1 — Flagging a business risk to senior leadership

> Hi [name],
>
> I'm flagging a potential impact to Q4 revenue. Several high-value customer meetings — including [customer1], [customer2], and [customer3] — were planned with my direct involvement at Team. My exclusion from the attendee list puts those engagements at risk. I've documented the pipeline impact in a Confluence page linked here.
>
> The ask: please review the attendee list and assess whether an exception is warranted. I'm available to discuss at your convenience.
>
> Thanks,
> Sarah

### Example 2 — Recommending a course of action to a peer leader

> Hi Adam,
>
> I want to align on when your AI SA team should and shouldn't be engaged in customer situations without an active sales opportunity. I've seen a pattern emerging — a CSM is routing requests to your team that, in my assessment, don't meet the qualification bar and would be better served by other resources.
>
> I'd recommend a brief conversation to align on qualification criteria. That way we can protect your team's capacity for the highest-value engagements. Happy to find 30 minutes this week.
>
> Thanks,
> Sarah

### Example 3 — Executive customer outreach (strategic account)

> Hi [Executive Name],
>
> Thank you for making time for our conversation on [date]. Based on what you shared, I see a clear opportunity for Atlassian to support [Company]'s goals around [key priority]. I'd like to propose a focused follow-up with our [relevant team/SME] to go deeper on [specific area].
>
> Would [date/time options] work on your end? I'll keep it to 30 minutes and come prepared with specific recommendations.
>
> Best regards,
> Sarah Petrie
> Principal Solutions Engineer, Atlassian

## 🤖 Prompting Tips for Rovo

When asking Rovo Dev to draft an executive message, provide:

- **Who** you're writing to (name, title, level)
- **The core issue or opportunity** in one sentence
- **The business impact** (revenue, risk, timeline, customer name)
- **Your recommended action** or what you need from them
- Any **constraints** (urgency, sensitivities, prior context)

**Example prompt:**

> "Using the Executive voice, draft a message to my VP flagging that a key customer account is at risk due to a delayed response from our Trust team. The customer is [customer], the issue is blocking their Rovo evaluation, and I need leadership visibility to accelerate the response."
